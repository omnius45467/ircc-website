Introducing the California Roofers University

The IRCC California Roofers University© has been established to assist “meritshop” roofing contractors keep their field management on pace with the growing public demand for “competent person” accountability and oversight in the execution of construction projects. With the advent of increased environmental regulations, coupled with expanding requirements for workers’ safety — i.e. fallprotection regulations, heat illness, exposure to hazardous materials and potential public exposure to toxic emissions, to name just a few — the bar continues to rise for contractors who wish to be accepted to the public bidding process, as well as a rising number of ‘exposure sensitive’ private entities who require the highest standards from those contractors who wish to work. While graduating a worker from an approved State or Federal apprenticeship program is critical, it is still short of the mark when addressing the growing number of bid pre‐requisites now being mandated by public agencies and some larger commercial property owners, or managers. Be prepared by enrolling your journeymen in the IRCC’s post‐apprenticeship Roofers University© advanced certification program for working field managers. Don’t be excluded from the bidding process by holding back on training for your crew leadership.

Apprenticeship Program

developing skilled, safe workforce

promoting high standards

fostering a training culture

ensuring sound value for roofing customers

The Independent Roofing Contractors’ Roofers Apprenticeship Program is the longest operating state approved “merit shop” training program in California. Sponsored by the IRCC association of roofing contractors, the apprenticeship school first began indenturing apprentices in 1992. Since then, the IRCC has successfully graduated hundreds of skilled journeymen and women into the California roofing industry. For an IRCC apprentice to graduate, it takes 3 ½ years to complete the program, and consists of seven levels of six months each, totaling 4,000 hrs. of OJT, in addition to approximately 144 hrs. per year of related classroom and 'hands‐on' labwork. Moving up these levels is a matter of passing an employer performance review, satisfactory completion of classes, and completing required OJT hours. In addition to offering state apprenticeship certification, the IRCC provides a Federal apprenticeship program through the Federal DOL — Department of Apprenticeship. Both the state and federal programs operate as one program in so far as the actual training. On‐the‐job, classroom, hands‐on labs and work book study requires no additional payment to the IRCC Training Trust by the participating roofing contractor when subscribing to both the State and Federal programs.There is no charge to individual apprentices for their participation in the training program. It is paid 100% by participating employers (contractors). The IRCC Training Trust covers training benefit. Fringe benefits such aspension and / or health insurance are the responsibility of subscribing employers.

A Culture of Learning

The Independent Roofing Contractors of California, Inc. Is committed to the proposition that training and skill development of the workforce is absolutely essential; and that any roofing contractor who does not foster or perpetuate a workplace “culture” committed to training and safety is destined to fail. Ongoing training translates into more opportunities and work hours for individual apprentices and journeymen. Ultimately, a training based culture extends the benefit of training to the commercial, governmental or residential customer.

Contractor Acceptance

Public demand for quality roof systems is higher than ever. The same holds true for ‘quality’ roofing contractors. Commercial property owners, as well as public entity construction, contract managers are becoming increasingly wary of contractors who can’t deliver sound, safe and functional roof system solutions. Roofing litigation related to failed systems has become a thriving cottage industry, with attorneys leading the charge against sloppy work, and negligent disregard for job site safety, and the prevention of serious injuries; property losses or customer and public exposure to job created hazards.

After the Apprenticeship

Learning and growing as a professional roofing mechanic does not end with receipt of a journeyman certificate. The next step is when and where it really gets interesting; and rewarding. Now it’s time to get into details of crew leadership, job planning, material acquisition, timing, staging and coordination of a successful, safe, and profitable operation. Getting this right translates into greater job security, income and benefits; and it opens opportunities for career growth which can expand pathways into company management, sales, technical consulting, teaching and marketing. That’s where the IRCC’s Roofers University comes in: developing well rounded field managers and supervisors who understand the full scope of running a job and the technical execution of the installation, from set‐up and tear off, to the final inspection.
